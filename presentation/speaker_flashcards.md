# Speaker Flashcards — 3-Minute Presentation
## SANDAG Regional Planning Committee
### AI-Driven Traffic Signal Optimization

> Total time: **3 minutes (180 seconds)**
> Pacing: ~30 seconds per slide
> Tone: Professional, policy-aware, technically informed, respectful

---

---

## CARD 1 — Title Slide
**⏱ ~15 seconds | Tone: Calm, grounded opening**

**Say:**
> "Thank you for the opportunity to present today.
> My name is Samarth Vaka, and I'd like to share a concept I've been developing
> around AI-driven traffic signal optimization — and why I believe it's relevant to
> SANDAG's regional planning work."

**Emphasis:** "relevant to SANDAG's regional planning work" — connect immediately to their mission.

**Pacing:** Slow, deliberate. Let the room settle. No rushing.

---

---

## CARD 2 — The Problem: Traffic Congestion
**⏱ ~35 seconds | Tone: Factual, build urgency**

**Say:**
> "Traffic congestion has measurable costs that extend well beyond inconvenience.
> Drivers in the U.S. spend roughly 7 to 10 percent of their trip time stopped at intersections.
> That translates to 6 billion gallons of fuel wasted annually at traffic lights —
> and around 28 million metric tons of CO₂.
>
> To put this in global perspective — Lagos was ranked the world's most congested city in 2025,
> where commuters lose roughly 30 hours per week to traffic. That's not just inconvenient.
> That is a structural failure of urban mobility."

**Emphasis:** "structural failure of urban mobility" — signals this is a serious planning issue, not a minor annoyance.

**Pacing:** Move through the statistics at a measured pace. Don't rush numbers. Pause after the Lagos line.

---

---

## CARD 3 — Intersections as Bottlenecks
**⏱ ~30 seconds | Tone: Analytical, reframe the problem**

**Say:**
> "Road expansion is often discussed as the solution to congestion.
> But even on wider roads, traffic throughput is ultimately constrained by intersections.
>
> Most signals today operate on fixed timing schedules — they follow a predetermined clock
> regardless of actual traffic demand. And when signals are optimized in isolation,
> the result is stop-and-go patterns that ripple across entire corridors.
>
> The bottleneck is not the road. It is signal coordination."

**Emphasis:** "The bottleneck is not the road. It is signal coordination." — speak this slowly and clearly. It reframes the conversation.

**Pacing:** The final sentence is your pivot. Let it land. Brief pause before moving on.

---

---

## CARD 4 — The Concept
**⏱ ~35 seconds | Tone: Confident, clear, technically credible**

**Say:**
> "The concept I've been exploring is an AI-based framework for signal timing optimization.
>
> The approach is straightforward: ingest traffic flow data and intersection delay patterns,
> apply machine learning to identify inefficiencies — recurring congestion cycles, unbalanced
> signal phases, poor coordination between intersections — and then generate optimized
> timing recommendations.
>
> Critically, this is a software-driven approach. It does not require significant new
> hardware infrastructure. The investment is in data analysis and modeling — not
> new construction."

**Emphasis:** "software-driven" and "not new construction" — these are reassuring to a planning committee. Cost sensitivity matters.

**Pacing:** The three-step process (ingest, analyze, recommend) should feel clear and logical. Don't speed through it.

---

---

## CARD 5 — Simulation Work
**⏱ ~25 seconds | Tone: Evidence-based, credible, modest**

**Say:**
> "To explore this concept, I developed a multi-intersection traffic simulation
> that benchmarks ten different signal control strategies — ranging from fixed timing
> baselines to adaptive and AI-driven approaches.
>
> The simulation evaluates performance across intersection delay, corridor travel time,
> and traffic throughput. The results demonstrate that strategy choice has a meaningful
> impact on performance — and that adaptive approaches show real potential.
>
> The code and results are available for review."

**Emphasis:** "The code and results are available for review." — this signals you are not just theorizing. You have done the work.

**Pacing:** Keep this slide brisk. The point is not to explain the simulation in depth — it is to establish credibility. Move confidently.

---

---

## CARD 6 — Regional Relevance & Next Steps
**⏱ ~40 seconds | Tone: Respectful, forward-looking, inviting**

**Say:**
> "I want to close by connecting this to SANDAG's role specifically.
>
> Major traffic corridors in San Diego County cross city and jurisdictional boundaries.
> Optimizing signal timing at scale requires someone with regional authority and a regional view.
> That is SANDAG.
>
> I am not proposing that this be implemented immediately. What I am proposing is that it
> be evaluated. A staff review, an analysis of a candidate pilot corridor, or a follow-up
> technical conversation could determine whether this concept has practical merit within
> SANDAG's planning work.
>
> I would welcome the opportunity to share the simulation work in more detail,
> or to discuss how this concept could potentially be evaluated in a small pilot corridor.
>
> Thank you."

**Emphasis:**
- "That is SANDAG." — a direct, confident attribution of regional authority. Not flattery — recognition.
- "I am not proposing that this be implemented immediately." — lower the stakes. This disarms resistance.
- Final invitation line: speak it clearly and calmly. Do not rush it. It is the most important sentence in the presentation.

**Pacing:** The closing should feel measured and unhurried. The final line should be the slowest delivery of the entire presentation.

**After "Thank you":** Hold eye contact. Do not immediately sit down or look away. Let the silence land for one beat.

## Tone Reminders

- You are presenting a **planning concept**, not a completed product.
- Speak as someone who has done rigorous work and wants to share it — not as someone seeking approval.
- Avoid hedge phrases like "I think maybe" or "this might possibly." Be direct.
- Avoid overselling. Phrases like "this will revolutionize" will undercut credibility.
- If a committee member asks a technical question you cannot fully answer: *"That is a great question — and one that a staff technical review could help answer more fully."*
