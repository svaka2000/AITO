## SLIDE 2 — The Problem: Traffic Congestion

**Congestion has real costs — economic and environmental**

- Drivers spend **7–10% of total trip time** waiting at intersections
- Average signal wait: **60–90 seconds** — often exceeding 2 minutes
- U.S. vehicles waste **~6 billion gallons of fuel** annually at traffic lights
- That produces roughly **28 million metric tons of CO₂** per year
- Global scale: Lagos ranked the world's most congested city in 2025 — commuters spend **~30 hours per week** in traffic

## SLIDE 3 — The Bottleneck: Intersections

**Roads can be widened. Intersections cannot be bypassed.**

- Intersections manage simultaneous demands: straight traffic, left turns, right turns, pedestrians, cyclists
- Most operate on **fixed signal cycles** — predetermined schedules that ignore real-time demand
- Signals optimized in isolation create **stop-and-go patterns across entire corridors**
- The limiting factor in urban traffic throughput is not road capacity — it is **signal coordination**

## SLIDE 4 — The Concept: AI-Based Signal Optimization

**A software-first approach to smarter signal timing**

The framework would:
1. **Ingest** traffic flow data, delay patterns, and corridor travel times
2. **Analyze** using machine learning to identify inefficient signal patterns
3. **Recommend** optimized signal timing schedules

ML models can detect:
- Recurring congestion cycles
- Unbalanced signal phases
- Poor coordination between intersections

> **Key advantage:** Analysis-driven optimization requires minimal new infrastructure.
> The investment is in software and data — not hardware.

## SLIDE 5 — Simulation Work

**Feasibility demonstrated through independent simulation**

- Developed a multi-intersection traffic simulation environment
- Benchmarked **10 signal control strategies** across multiple performance metrics
- Evaluated: intersection delay, corridor travel time, and traffic throughput
- Compared fixed timing baselines against adaptive and AI-driven approaches
- Results show meaningful performance differences between strategies under varying demand conditions
